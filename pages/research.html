How This Calculator Works

A simple, research‑based way to figure out exactly how many minutes to run each sprinkler zone—so you save water, money, and keep your lawn green.

In short: We look at how much water your grass actually needs based on the weather (FAO‑56 Penman–Monteith ET), subtract effective rainfall, and translate what’s left into minutes using your system’s output rate. This gives you right‑sized runtimes per zone.

The Exact Formula Used

The calculator uses the following formula to determine runtime per irrigation event:

Runtime (minutes) = (W ÷ 7) × (ET × 0.75 − R) × Rate
  • W = watering frequency in days (e.g., water every 3 days)
  • ET = weekly evapotranspiration in inches for your location
  • R = weekly total rainfall in inches for your location
  • Rate = minutes it takes your sprinklers to apply 1 inch of water

Plain English version:
Start with how much water the weather says your lawn used this week, take 75% of that amount (since most lawns do well without replacing 100%), subtract the rainfall, adjust based on how often you water, and then convert inches into minutes with your system’s rate.

1) Weather Tells Us How Thirsty Your Grass Is

Grass loses water each day through heat, sun, and wind. Scientists call this ET (short for evapotranspiration). Public weather networks publish ET values so we know how much water grass used in your area.

Studies show you don’t need to replace 100% of that water to keep a healthy lawn. Most home lawns look great with about 70–80% of ET replaced in summer, and 60–70% in spring/fall. We default to 75% as a practical, water‑saving baseline.

2) Giving Credit for Rainfall

When it rains, your sprinklers can take a break. But not all rain soaks into the soil—some runs off or evaporates. That’s why we only count the portion that actually benefits the roots, commonly called effective rainfall. The calculator subtracts weekly rainfall before converting the remainder into minutes.

3) How Fast Your Sprinklers Apply Water

Every sprinkler system puts out water at a certain speed. The calculator asks for your minutes per inch value (Rate). You can figure this out by:

  • Cup test (best way): Place a few cups on the lawn, run sprinklers for 15–20 minutes, and measure how much water collected. From this, calculate how many minutes it takes to apply 1 inch.
  • Specs/math: You can use gallons per minute and area formulas, but real‑world testing is more reliable (especially with mixed heads and pressure differences).

4) Putting It All Together

Following this formula, the result is the number of minutes per zone you should run your sprinklers. If that time is long, split it into two or three shorter “cycle‑and‑soak” sessions to reduce runoff and improve absorption.

Example

Let’s say the weather shows your lawn used about 1.1 inches of water this week. Multiply that by 0.75 (75% replacement) = 0.83 inches. It rained 0.1 inch, leaving 0.73 inches still needed. If your system applies 1 inch in 60 minutes and you water every 3 days (W = 3), then per watering: (3 ÷ 7) × 0.73 × 60 ≈ 19 minutes.

Why This Works

This approach is backed by decades of turfgrass and irrigation research. Universities and organizations like the Irrigation Association and EPA WaterSense recommend weather‑based irrigation with appropriate deficits to maintain healthy turf while conserving water.
